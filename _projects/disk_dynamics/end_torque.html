from scipy.interpolate import interp1d
from scipy.stats import sem 


import warnings
warnings.filterwarnings('ignore')

def get_torque(base, n_max=50, suffix=''):
    a_std=np.linspace(1.01, 1.99, 1000)
    tauc=np.zeros(n_max)
    tauc_sign=np.zeros(n_max)
    for ii in range(n_max):
        dat2=np.genfromtxt(base+'_{0}'.format(ii+1)+suffix)
        order=np.argsort(dat2[:,0])
        dat2=dat2[order]
        dat2[:,1]=np.cumsum(dat2[:,1])
        tauc[ii]=(interp1d(dat2[:,0], np.abs(dat2[:,1]))(a_std[-1]))
        tauc_sign[ii]=(interp1d(dat2[:,0], dat2[:,1])(a_std[-1]))
        
    return np.mean(tauc),sem(tauc), np.mean(tauc_sign)

Consider a test orbit embedded inside of an eccentric disk. The semi-major axis distribution within the disk is $dN/da \propto a^{-1}$ extending from $a=1$ to $a=2$. The disk contains 1000 particles with a small spread in their angular momentum and eccentricity vectors ($\sim 1-2^{\circ}$). All orbits have an eccentricity of 0.7.

The plot below shows the timescale to change the angular momentum of the test orbit as a function of its semi-major axis ($a_{\rm test}$) and its orientation relative to an eccentric nuclear disk ($\bar{\omega}$). This time-scale is defined as

$$t_j\equiv\frac{j_{\rm test}}{\overline{|\tau_z|}},$$

where $\tau_z$ is the z-component of the torque on the test orbit and $j_{\rm test}$ is its angular momentum. $\overline{|\tau_z|}$ is average of the absolute value of the torque on the test orbit over 50 realizations of the disk. The different symbols (diamonds and circles) indicate sign of the torque. Note that the sign of the torque is the same in each realization, except when $\bar{\omega}=0$.

The dashed grey line is the secular timescale within the disk.

We have normalized $t_j$ by the test orbit's period, which decreases with semi-major axis. However, even without this normalization, $t_j$ is still much longer for smaller smas (roghly speaking $\tau \propto a^3$ and $t_j\propto a^{-2.5}$).
